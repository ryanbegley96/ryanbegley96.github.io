About me

I am an astrophysicist studying how the earliest galaxies shaped the universe. My research focuses on how young, star-forming galaxies produce and escape ionizing radiation, which is key to understanding cosmic reionization. I work with observations from JWST and leading ground-based telescopes such as the VLT and have involvements to major international projects like PRIMER, VANDELS, and EXCELS.

I obtained my PhD in Astronomy, supervised by Prof. Ross McLure, Dr. Fergus Cullen and Prof. Jim Dunlop, from the University of Edinburgh in 2023. My thesis was titled "Understanding cosmic reionization: the escape fraction of Lyman-continuum and Lyman-alpha photons in high-redshift galaxies" and can be found here.

The evolution of [O III]+Hβ equivalent width from z≃3−8
Implications for the production and escape of ionizing photons during reionization

In this 2025 paper, we explore how the emission lines from oxygen and hydrogen (specifically, [OIII] and Hβ) evolve in galaxies over a wide range of redshifts, from when the universe was about 2.5 billion years old to just a few hundred million years after the Big Bang (z ≃ 3 to z ≃ 8). These emission lines are valuable tools for understanding star formation and the production of ionizing photons, which are crucial to the process of cosmic reionization — the era when the universe transitioned from opaque to transparent.

By examining galaxies observed with the James Webb Space Telescope (JWST) through the PRIMER and JADES programs, we found that the equivalent width of these emission lines (a measure of their strength relative to the galaxy's continuum light) increases significantly as we look further back in time. Specifically, we saw a jump in the median [OIII] + Hβ equivalent widths from around 310 Å at z ≃ 3 to over 540 Å at z ≃ 7, suggesting that star formation in these early galaxies was changing, likely becoming more intense or bursty over time.

We also observed interesting patterns in how the [OIII] + Hβ equivalent widths correlate with galaxy properties like stellar mass and UV brightness. We found that galaxies with lower UV luminosities and higher masses tend to have weaker emission lines. This suggests that galaxies with stronger emission lines are likely more capable of producing the ionizing radiation needed to contribute to reionization. However, our findings also suggest that while some galaxies are significant contributors of ionizing radiation, most of the reionization was driven by galaxies with more moderate levels of photon production. This aligns with current models that suggest a diverse range of galaxies contributed to reionization without overproducing ionizing photons.

In essence, this paper highlights the role of high-redshift galaxies in shaping the universe as we know it today, helping us understand the conditions that allowed the first stars and galaxies to emerge and the universe to become transparent to light.
